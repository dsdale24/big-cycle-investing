# Dalio-framework review: big-cycle-investing

_Date: 2026-04-15_
_Branch at time of review: main_
_Reviewer: dalio (Claude subagent)_

**Headline:** The project has internalized Dalio's four-mode debt-cycle taxonomy and his scope-humility principle better than most Dalio-inspired work, but the operational machinery covers only one of Dalio's three scales and has not specced most of his eight empire drivers, his five-stage transition arc, or his stated wealth-preservation playbook.

## What's working

- **Four-mode debt-cycle resolution is named, not just invoked.** `specs/theses/bond-allocation.md` enumerates austerity / restructuring / default / monetization with per-mode bondholder outcomes, and `us-fiscal-deterioration.md` explicitly argues why mode 4 is the likely US resolution — matching Dalio's reasoning in *Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises* (2018), Part 1, "The Classic Deflationary Debt Cycle" / "The Classic Inflationary Debt Cycle," and his argument in *Changing World Order* (2021) Ch. 3 that reserve-currency issuers typically resolve via debasement.
- **Scope humility matches Dalio's own methodological stance.** `backtest-sample-scope.md` treats US 1975-present as a single-era sample and names UK 1900-1980, Weimar, France 1918-1928, Argentina, Japan 1990- as the honest transition-scale test set. This is exactly the multi-country, multi-century frame Dalio built *Changing World Order* on (Ch. 2, "The Big Cycle of the Last 500 Years"). Most Dalio-inspired portfolios skip this step.
- **AllWeather is correctly flagged as non-neutral.** `bond-allocation.md` notes AllWeather embodies mode-1 (disinflation) assumptions. This aligns with Dalio's own framing — All Weather was designed for a world where the four environments (growth up/down × inflation up/down) rotate cyclically, not for a reserve-currency transition. Dalio himself has said so in LinkedIn essays from 2019-2021.
- **The bond-holder matrix is Dalio's own.** The four-row table in `bond-allocation.md` (mode 1 win, modes 2-4 lose) mirrors the logic in *Big Debt Crises* Part 1 closely.

## Findings

### 1. Three-scale taxonomy diverges from Dalio's in a non-trivial way

The project uses **cyclical (3-10y) / secular (10-40y) / transition (50-200y)**. Dalio's canonical structure (*Changing World Order* Ch. 2, *Big Debt Crises* Part 1) is:

- Short-term debt cycle: ~5-8 years (recessions / recoveries driven by credit expansion-contraction)
- Long-term debt cycle: ~50-75 years (deleveraging cycles; the 1930s and 2008 are the last two US instances)
- Reserve-currency / empire arc: ~150-250 years (Dutch, British, US)

The project's "secular" bucket (10-40y) does not cleanly map to Dalio's long-term debt cycle (~50-75y). It sits in the gap between his short-term and long-term cycles. Consequence: the project has no natural bucket for the thing Dalio thinks matters most besides the empire arc — the big leverage cycle that ends in a debt-restructuring / debasement episode roughly once a human lifetime. Neither the US 1930s nor 2008 sit cleanly in "secular" as defined. This reframing also means the `us-fiscal-deterioration` thesis (which spans both buckets) has to straddle two scales in the project's vocabulary that Dalio treats as one.

### 2. Five-stage reserve-currency decline arc is not specced

*Changing World Order* Ch. 4 decomposes the empire arc into stages: (1) rise through education / innovation / productivity, (2) peak, (3) debt buildup and external-military overextension, (4) internal-order breakdown (inequality, polarization, civil conflict), (5) external-order breakdown (great-power conflict, currency displacement). This is the load-bearing structure of Dalio's framework — it's where his "where are we now" calls come from.

The project has indicators touching stages 1 (`indicator_framework.md` §7 education), 3 (debt/GDP, deficit trajectory), 4 (Gini, EPU, sentiment in `internal_order_stress_index`), and 5 (reserve share, military spend in `indicator_framework.md` §8). But there is no artifact — thesis, spec, or indicator composite — that **attempts to locate where the US is on Dalio's five-stage arc**. The pieces are in the data pipeline; the framework that Dalio's whole argument is built around is not operationalized. This is the single biggest framework-alignment gap.

### 3. Eight empire drivers: four covered, four missing or weak

Dalio's eight drivers of empire rise-and-decline (*Changing World Order* Ch. 1 and Appendix): **education, competitiveness, innovation/technology, economic output, share of world trade, military, financial-center status, reserve-currency status.**

Project coverage:

| Driver | Project coverage |
|---|---|
| Education | Specced in §7 but "lowest priority for backtesting" — not operationalized |
| Competitiveness | Not specced; no direct indicator |
| Innovation / technology | Patent applications listed in §7 but not in any composite |
| Economic output | Real GDP, US share of world GDP (§8) — covered |
| Share of world trade | Trade balance, current account — covered |
| Military | Military spending vs China — listed but not composited |
| Financial-center status | Not specced (e.g., share of global IPOs, cross-border lending, FX turnover) |
| Reserve-currency status | USD share of global reserves (§8) — covered |

The four covered drivers are exactly the ones FRED makes easy. The four missing or weak drivers are the ones that require cross-dataset work (SIPRI, WIPO, BIS, WEF competitiveness index). In *Changing World Order* Ch. 1 Dalio explicitly argues the composite of all eight is what matters — no single one is decisive. A project that tracks half of them and composites none of them is not using the framework as constructed.

### 4. Wealth-preservation playbook is discussed but not directly implemented

Dalio's explicit late-empire playbook (*Changing World Order* Ch. 14-15, "Investing Principles" and "The Future"): hold **gold, inflation-linked bonds, productive real assets, non-reserve-currency equities, selective commodities**; underweight long-duration nominal sovereign debt of the declining reserve issuer.

Project state:

- `BigCycleStrategy.BASE_PROFILES["non_sovereign_heavy"]` holds 30% equities / 5% long bonds / 5% short bonds / 25% gold / 20% commodities / 15% cash. Matches Dalio's direction on bonds and gold but is all-US-equity; no non-USD equity sleeve.
- TIPS is not an asset class in `ASSET_CLASSES = [equities, long_bonds, short_bonds, gold, commodities, cash]`, despite being Dalio's named mode-4 hedge and being listed in `indicator_framework.md` §9. Issue #10 tracks this; it's been open since 2026-04-13.
- "Productive real assets" (real estate, infrastructure, resource producers as distinct from commodity futures) are not in the asset taxonomy.
- Non-USD equities / ex-US equities — not in the asset taxonomy.

The project has operationalized Dalio's bond-aversion but not Dalio's affirmative wealth-preservation basket. The `non_sovereign_heavy` PR #56 result (Sharpe 0.39 vs 0.84) reflects a concentrated gold+commodities bet, not a Dalio-shaped basket. That is a relevant distinction when interpreting the negative result: it says something about static real-asset concentration, not about Dalio's actual prescription.

### 5. AllWeather comparator is used more than its caveat tolerates

`bond-allocation.md` documents that AllWeather embodies mode-1 assumptions and is not neutral. But every evidence-log entry in `bond-allocation.md`, `regime-aware-allocation.md`, and `docs/research/regime_scoring_comparison.md` benchmarks against it. The framework-consistent approach is to either (a) replace it with a Dalio-shaped ex-US gold+real-assets basket, or (b) run Dalio's own four-environment decomposition and report how each strategy fared in each environment (that's how Bridgewater itself reports, per Dalio's 2011 "Engineering Targeted Returns and Risks" whitepaper). Issue #62 flags this but the pattern continues.

### 6. Civilizational-leads-financial thesis misses Dalio's own operationalization

The project's `internal_order_stress_index` uses **Gini + EPU + consumer sentiment**. Dalio's internal-order index in *Changing World Order* Ch. 5 uses a different and arguably more transition-relevant basket: **wealth gap × values gap × populism / political polarization × internal conflict events × debt burden**. The project's choice of EPU + sentiment brings in two fast-reacting components that `docs/research/civilizational_component_decomposition.md` correctly identified as causing the composite to lag at cyclical scale. A Dalio-faithful composite would replace EPU and sentiment with populism measures (vote share for anti-establishment parties), polarization (DW-NOMINATE, already mentioned in the indicator spec but not implemented), and explicit debt-burden — all slow-moving. Issue #60 flags the reweight but hasn't been actioned.

### 7. External-order arc is indicator-level, not narrative-level

Dalio's external-order framework (*Changing World Order* Ch. 6) tracks a coalition dynamic: rising power forms alternative alliances, declining power's sanctions lose bite, reserve-currency alternatives coalesce. `us-fiscal-deterioration.md` names this briefly ("SWIFT weaponization," "alliance stress") and marks it as needing a news-monitoring function (issue #59, deferred). Until that exists, the external-order side of Dalio's framework is only tracked through the quantitative residue (reserve share, military spend) — the dynamics Dalio treats as primary are not measured.

### 8. Cross-national empirical discipline is correctly identified as the honest bar but not pursued

`backtest-sample-scope.md` names this plainly. The 2026-04-15 external review named #52 as load-bearing-but-stalled. Dalio's own methodological move — what makes *Changing World Order* different from its predecessors — is the cross-national multi-century dataset. A project that adopts his framework but runs on one country × 50 years inherits the conclusions without the evidence that warranted them. The project specs know this; the work hasn't shifted to match.

## The framework-blind-spot question

Dalio's framework itself is silent on:

1. **Technology regime shifts that aren't resource-exhaustion-driven.** The printing-press / Reformation arc the project captures in `institutional-adaptation.md` is *the project's own addition* to Dalio — and it's a good addition. Dalio's framework treats AI adoption mostly as a productivity-growth input (see his 2024 LinkedIn essays); it doesn't have the coalition-scrambling, institutional-rigidity mode-of-failure the project names. The project is ahead of the framework on this one.

2. **Private / non-state currency competition.** Dalio's reserve-currency framework treats the contenders as other states' fiat currencies (RMB, EUR). Crypto is mentioned in his 2021 essays but not integrated into the five-stage arc. The project inherits this blind spot — there's no thesis or indicator for private-currency share of global settlement or stablecoin-denominated Treasury holdings. This may or may not matter; if it matters it will become obvious over the next decade.

3. **Geopolitical discontinuities that don't match historical patterns.** Dalio's method is explicitly pattern-matching against the last 500 years. A discontinuity — bioweapon release, AI takeoff that restructures who produces what, climate-driven mass migration at a scale not seen in the dataset — is outside the method by construction. The project inherits this; nothing wrong with that, but worth flagging that even a perfectly Dalio-faithful project is fragile to this class.

## Recommendations

1. **Spec the five-stage arc as an interpretation overlay.** A single markdown artifact (`specs/theses/reserve-currency-arc-position.md` or similar) that names each stage, cites the indicators that bear on it, and states a current-best-guess position for the US. This doesn't require new data — it requires assembling what exists into Dalio's own frame. This is the cheapest high-leverage move.

2. **Compose the four missing empire drivers.** Financial-center status, competitiveness, innovation/tech, military — each has freely available data (BIS for financial-center, WEF for competitiveness, WIPO for patents, SIPRI for military). An eight-driver composite with its own evidence log would let the project make Dalio-style "where are we" calls.

3. **Add TIPS and ex-US equity to the asset taxonomy.** Issue #10 is 2 days old; it doesn't need a spec rewrite, it needs a data fetch + test. Until these exist, the "Dalio basket" the project is comparing against isn't Dalio's basket.

4. **Replace the internal-order composite per Dalio's own recipe.** Issue #60 is open; the component decomposition (PR #53) already showed EPU+sentiment are the lagging components. Swapping them for DW-NOMINATE polarization and populist-vote-share is both framework-faithful and technically cleaner.

## Questions worth sitting with

1. Is the project trying to apply Dalio's framework, or to build a Dalio-inspired framework that diverges where the evidence warrants? The scale taxonomy divergence and the institutional-adaptation thesis both suggest the latter. If that's the frame, it should be stated — "Dalio-adjacent, with these explicit departures" is more defensible than "Dalio-faithful" when the departures are this many.

2. What is the smallest artifact that would make the five-stage arc operational? A single quarterly-updated markdown with eight bullet points and a "current best guess" one-liner? Or a full composite index? The marginal value of the former is probably high; the marginal value of the latter is unclear without the former existing first.

3. If cross-national data (#52) is the honest bar Dalio himself met, what is the specific blocker that makes it a 12-month-deferred item rather than the critical path? This question was asked in the 2026-04-15 external review and is still unanswered.
